QR Code Guide

What Is a QR Code and How Does It Work?

A QR code, short for Quick Response code, is a two-dimensional barcode that stores information in a pattern of dark and light modules. A compatible camera or QR scanner reads the pattern and converts it into data such as a web address, text, contact information or network configuration.

How to Create a QR Code

Choose the type of content you want to encode, enter the required information, select your preferred size and error correction level, and then generate the QR code. You can customize the foreground and background colors as long as there is enough contrast for reliable scanning.

QR Codes for Websites

URL QR codes are useful for posters, menus, packaging, business cards, product displays and event materials. Before publishing a QR code, scan it with multiple devices and confirm that the destination opens correctly.

QR Codes for WiFi Sharing

A WiFi QR code can make guest network access more convenient. It can contain the network name, security type and password. Only encode network credentials when you understand that anyone who can scan or access the printed code may be able to retrieve the information.

Choosing Error Correction

QR codes support different levels of error correction. Higher levels can tolerate more damage but may require a denser code. For ordinary digital sharing, a moderate level can be a practical choice. For printed materials that may become dirty or damaged, higher correction may be useful if the resulting QR code remains easy to scan.

Why Contrast Matters

QR scanners need to distinguish the modules from the background. Dark modules on a light background are a classic choice because they provide strong contrast. Avoid low-contrast combinations and test your QR code under the actual lighting and print conditions where it will be used.

Do QR Codes Expire?

A static QR code itself does not have an expiration date. However, the information it points to can change. For example, a QR code containing a URL will continue to encode that URL, but the website may later move, be removed or stop responding.

Static vs Dynamic QR Codes

A static QR code stores its final information directly. A dynamic QR code usually points to a redirect service that can change the destination without replacing the printed code. This page creates static QR codes. If you need campaign analytics or editable destinations, you may need a dedicated dynamic QR platform.

Best Practices for Printing QR Codes

Use sufficient physical size, strong contrast and an uncluttered quiet zone. Avoid stretching the QR image disproportionately. Test the printed version from the distance and angle your audience will use. For important materials, test multiple phone models and scanning applications.

QR Codes and Privacy

Remember that a QR code is simply a convenient data container. If you encode personal information, contact details or WiFi credentials, consider who can access the code. For sensitive data, use appropriate security controls rather than treating a QR code as a security mechanism.
